Russian packs grammar inside the word: one lemma appears in dozens of inflected forms. A good tokenizer factors each form into a shared root plus a short ending — instead of memorizing every form whole, or worse, shattering Cyrillic into raw bytes. Finding 01

Not knowing the script is the dominant cost

Before any linguistics, a tokenizer that never learned Cyrillic fragments Russian into bytes — and because every Cyrillic letter is two UTF-8 bytes, it pays an order-of-magnitude premium over a tokenizer that learned the script.

Why it happens

The gap is two effects stacked:

×2 — encoding. A byte-level fallback spends two tokens per Cyrillic character before modeling anything.

×4.3 — subword learning. Learned merges pack characters into morpheme- and word-sized units, taking fertility from 5.93 down to 1.39. Together: an 8.6× coverage penalty that a Russian-specific tokenizer erases outright.

Finding 02

Low fertility isn’t morphological competence

Give BPE a generous vocabulary and its fertility drops toward one token per word — but it gets there by memorizing whole word-forms, not by isolating morphemes. Unigram emits slightly more tokens yet reuses the root across a paradigm, so it needs fewer distinct token types — the property that generalizes to unseen forms.

How it’s built

A small, fully reproducible harness

Everything here runs from raw text in seconds. The point isn’t a state-of-the-art tokenizer — it’s an honest, legible measurement of two real effects, on a corpus engineered to stress exactly them.
